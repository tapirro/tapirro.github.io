Muña: The Andean Mint That Fights H. pylori

At market stalls throughout the Peruvian highlands, bundles of aromatic muña (Minthostachys mollis) are sold as casually as basil or parsley. This Andean mint has been used for centuries as a digestive remedy — and modern microbiology is beginning to explain why.

Traditional Uses

In traditional Andean medicine, muña tea is the first-line treatment for soroche (altitude sickness), stomach cramps, and digestive complaints. Shepherds chew the leaves to stay warm at high altitudes. The dried herb is burned as a fumigant to repel insects from grain stores.

What makes muña particularly interesting is its specificity: it's used for digestive infections, not just general stomach upset. This suggests traditional practitioners recognized its antimicrobial properties long before laboratory confirmation.

What Research Shows

A 2014 study published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology demonstrated that muña essential oil had significant bactericidal activity against Helicobacter pylori, the bacterium responsible for most stomach ulcers and a major risk factor for gastric cancer.

The key active compounds are pulegone (40-60% of essential oil) and menthone (15-25%). These monoterpenes disrupt bacterial cell membranes, showing activity against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.

A 2019 study from Universidad Nacional Mayor de San Marcos showed that muña extract inhibited 8 out of 10 tested bacterial strains at concentrations achievable through traditional tea preparation.

Safety Warning

Here's the critical caveat: pulegone, muña's primary bioactive compound, is hepatotoxic at high doses. Pennyroyal oil (which also contains pulegone) has caused fatal liver damage when consumed in concentrated form.

Occasional muña tea is considered safe by toxicological standards. But concentrated essential oil ingestion is dangerous. This distinction between traditional preparation (tea) and modern concentrated extracts is crucial.
