Lucuma: The Low-Glycemic Fruit the Incas Called Gold

If you've eaten ice cream in Peru, you've probably tasted lucuma (Pouteria lucuma). This orange-fleshed Andean fruit has a flavor often described as a cross between maple syrup, sweet potato, and caramel — and it's been used as a natural sweetener for thousands of years.

Why Lucuma Matters for Health

In a world searching for sugar alternatives, lucuma stands out for a biochemical reason: its glycemic index is remarkably low. Despite its intense sweetness, lucuma powder has a GI of approximately 25 (compared to sugar's 65 and honey's 58).

This is because lucuma's sweetness comes not primarily from simple sugars but from a combination of starches, fibers, and sugar alcohols that are digested more slowly. For people managing blood sugar — or anyone looking to reduce refined sugar intake — this makes lucuma a genuinely useful ingredient.

Nutritional Content

Lucuma is rich in beta-carotene (provitamin A), giving it that distinctive orange color. It also provides notable amounts of iron (2.4mg per 100g of dried powder), zinc, niacin (vitamin B3), and dietary fiber.

The antioxidant profile includes xanthophylls and phenolic compounds, with in vitro studies showing free radical scavenging activity comparable to that of blueberries.

Clinical Evidence

Research on lucuma is still in early stages. A 2009 study from Universidad Nacional Agraria La Molina found that lucuma extract inhibited alpha-glucosidase — the enzyme that breaks down complex carbohydrates — suggesting a mechanism for its low glycemic effect.

A 2010 study in the Journal of Medicinal Food demonstrated wound-healing promotion in vitro, attributed to its beta-carotene and phenolic content. However, no large-scale human clinical trials have been published.

How It's Used

In Peru, lucuma is everywhere: ice cream, yogurt, cakes, smoothies, baby food. Outside South America, it's mostly available as a dried powder. The powder retains most of the fruit's nutritional profile and can substitute for sugar in baking at a roughly 2:1 ratio (lucuma:sugar).
