Why Herb-Drug Interactions Matter: A Guide for Supplement Users

If you take prescription medications and herbal supplements, there's something you need to know: herbs are not pharmacologically inert. They contain bioactive compounds that can interact with drugs — sometimes dangerously.

How Interactions Happen

Most herb-drug interactions occur through one of three mechanisms:

1. CYP450 enzyme modulation — The cytochrome P450 enzyme family metabolizes approximately 75% of all prescription drugs. Some herbs inhibit these enzymes (slowing drug metabolism and increasing blood levels), while others induce them (accelerating metabolism and reducing drug effectiveness). St. John's Wort is the classic example — it induces CYP3A4, potentially rendering birth control pills, HIV medications, and immunosuppressants ineffective.

2. P-glycoprotein interference — This transporter protein pumps drugs out of cells, affecting absorption and distribution. Herbs like grapefruit (technically a fruit, but the principle applies) inhibit P-gp, increasing drug absorption beyond intended levels.

3. Pharmacodynamic interactions — Herbs with similar effects to drugs can produce additive or synergistic effects. For example, combining garlic supplements (which have mild anticoagulant activity) with blood thinners like warfarin increases bleeding risk.

Andean Plants and Drug Interactions

Many Andean medicinal plants have documented or theoretical drug interactions:

- Cat's Claw may enhance anticoagulant effects and interact with immunosuppressants and antihypertensives

- Maca may interact with thyroid medications and hormone therapies

- Muña contains hepatotoxic pulegone that may compound liver stress from other medications

- Sacha Inchi's high omega-3 content may enhance anticoagulant effects

What You Should Do

First: always tell your doctor about supplements you take. Second: check for interactions before combining herbs with medications.
